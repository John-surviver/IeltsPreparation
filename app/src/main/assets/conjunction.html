A conjunction is a word that connects words, phrases, or clauses within a sentence. It helps to establish relationships, show the logical connections between different parts of a sentence, and coordinate or subordinate ideas.


Here are some common types of conjunctions along with examples:


1. Coordinating Conjunctions: Join words, phrases, or independent clauses of equal importance.

   Example: I like tea and coffee.


   Common coordinating conjunctions: and, but, or, so, for, nor, yet


2. Subordinating Conjunctions: Introduce subordinate clauses that depend on the main clause.

   Example: She went to the store because she needed groceries.


   Common subordinating conjunctions: because, if, although, when, while, until, since, before, after


3. Correlative Conjunctions: Come in pairs and join elements of equal importance.

   Example: Either you come with me or I'll go alone.


   Common correlative conjunctions: either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also, whether...or


4. Conjunctive Adverbs: Connect independent clauses or show relationships between ideas.

   Example: He studied hard; therefore, he passed the exam.


   Common conjunctive adverbs: however, therefore, moreover, nevertheless, consequently, meanwhile, furthermore


5. Subordinating Conjunctions of Time: Show the relationship of time between the main and subordinate clauses.

   Example: I will call you when I arrive home.


   Common subordinating conjunctions of time: when, while, before, after, since, until, as soon as


6. Subordinating Conjunctions of Place: Indicate the relationship of place between the main and subordinate clauses.

   Example: I found my keys where I left them.


   Common subordinating conjunctions of place: where, wherever


Conjunctions play a vital role in connecting and organizing ideas within a sentence. They help to create coherence, establish logical relationships, and improve the flow of information in writing or speech. By using conjunctions effectively, we can convey complex thoughts and express relationships between different elements of our language.
